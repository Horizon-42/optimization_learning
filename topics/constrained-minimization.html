Local minimization with constraints

Constrained minimization tutorial

Constrained minimization keeps the local-search goal of reducing a scalar objective, but every step must also respect bounds, equalities, and inequalities. This tutorial explains the feasible-set idea first, then shows how trust-constr and SLSQP encode the same constrained Rosenbrock problem in SciPy.

From Unconstrained To Feasible

In an unconstrained problem, a step is judged mostly by whether it lowers the objective \(f(x)\). In a constrained problem, lowering the objective is not enough. The point must also be feasible, meaning it satisfies the rules of the problem.

Start with a current point \(x\). The objective \(f(x)\) is still the scalar value you want to make small. Bounds restrict each variable directly. Constraint functions \(c(x)\) describe extra quantities that must stay between lower and upper limits:

\[ \begin{aligned} \min_x\quad & f(x),\\ c^\ell &\leq c(x)\leq c^u,\\ x^\ell &\leq x\leq x^u. \end{aligned} \]

The new object is the feasible set: all \(x\) values that satisfy those bounds and constraints. A good constrained step must improve the objective while staying in, or moving back toward, that feasible set.

Mental model: unconstrained minimization asks "which point is low?" Constrained minimization asks "which feasible point is low?"

How To Read Constraints

Bounds, inequalities, and equalities look similar in code, but they play different roles.

A bound is a direct limit on one coordinate:

\[ 0\leq x_0\leq 1. \]

An inequality leaves room. For example, \(x_0+2x_1\leq 1\) allows every point on one side of a line:

\[ x_0+2x_1\leq 1. \]

An equality removes more freedom. The condition \(2x_0+x_1=1\) forces the solution to lie exactly on a line:

\[ 2x_0+x_1=1. \]

The tempting shortcut is to ignore the constraints, minimize the objective, and then project the answer back into the allowed region. That can fail because the best feasible point is usually not the projection of the unconstrained minimum. The constraint geometry and the objective geometry have to be considered together.

The Tutorial Problem

The SciPy constrained examples use the two-variable Rosenbrock objective. By itself, Rosenbrock wants to reach the valley minimum near \((1,1)\). The constraints below change the question: among the points that obey the line, curve, and bound rules, which one has the lowest Rosenbrock value?

\[ \begin{aligned} \min_{x_0,x_1}\quad &100(x_1-x_0^2)^2+(1-x_0)^2,\\ x_0+2x_1&\leq1,\\ x_0^2+x_1&\leq1,\\ x_0^2-x_1&\leq1,\\ 2x_0+x_1&=1,\\ 0\leq x_0&\leq1,\\ -0.5\leq x_1&\leq2. \end{aligned} \]

Meaning: the optimizer is not free to chase the lowest contour anywhere in the plane. It must search along the feasible geometry carved out by the constraints.

trust-constr And SLSQP

Both methods are local constrained optimizers. They start from the same question: from the current point \(x_k\), what nearby point should be tried next if the objective wants to go one way and the constraints allow only some directions?

The tempting shortcut is to take an unconstrained step and then repair it afterward. That is often the wrong mental model. A repair step may land on a feasible point that is not low, or it may destroy the curvature information that made the original step useful. The constrained methods instead build feasibility into the step choice.

Near \(x_k\), a smooth objective can be approximated by a local quadratic model:

\[ m_k(d) = f(x_k) + \nabla f(x_k)^T d + \frac{1}{2}d^T H_k d. \]

Here \(d\) is the proposed step. The model \(m_k(d)\) is not the original objective; it is a cheaper local prediction of what would happen if we moved from \(x_k\) to \(x_k+d\). The matrix \(H_k\) represents curvature in that local model; depending on the solver setup, it may come from an exact Hessian, a Hessian-vector product, or a quasi-Newton approximation. The constraints are treated locally too, so each iteration solves a simpler constrained subproblem before deciding whether to accept the step.

trust-constr: trust the model only nearby

trust-constr adds one extra piece to the subproblem: a trust region. The solver does not let the local model make promises arbitrarily far away from \(x_k\). It asks for a step inside a ball of radius \(\Delta_k\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \min_d\quad & m_k(d),\\ \text{subject to}\quad &\text{local approximations of the constraints},\\ &\lVert d\rVert \leq \Delta_k. \end{aligned} \]

The radius \(\Delta_k\) is the solver's confidence knob. If the trial step behaves like the model predicted, the radius can grow. If the trial step gives poor progress or hurts feasibility too much, the radius shrinks and the next step must stay closer to \(x_k\).

Inequalities add another teaching idea: slack. A constraint such as \(c(x)\leq0\) can be rewritten with a positive slack variable \(s\):

\[ c(x)+s=0,\qquad s>0. \]

This lets an interior-point method work with equality-like equations while using a barrier term to keep \(s\) positive. The barrier starts as a strong warning against touching the boundary and is relaxed as the method gets closer to the solution.

The SciPy interface mirrors this "state the allowed interval" viewpoint. Bounds describes limits on the variables. LinearConstraint describes expressions of the form \(A x\). NonlinearConstraint describes expressions \(c(x)\). In both constraint objects, the key shape is the same:

\[ \text{lower bound}\leq \text{constraint value}\leq \text{upper bound}. \]

Equal lower and upper bounds mean equality. An infinite lower or upper bound means one side is absent. This is why trust-constr is a good teaching interface: the code looks like the mathematical problem statement.

SLSQP: solve a sequence of quadratic programs

SLSQP stands for Sequential Least Squares Programming. The important word for intuition is sequential. The method does not solve the nonlinear constrained problem in one jump. It repeatedly builds a quadratic approximation of the objective, linearizes the constraints, solves that easier constrained subproblem, and then updates the current point.

Read one SLSQP iteration as:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{nonlinear problem} &\rightarrow \text{quadratic objective model}\\ &\rightarrow \text{linearized constraints}\\ &\rightarrow \text{trial step}\\ &\rightarrow \text{new local model}. \end{aligned} \]

The dictionary interface uses a different sign convention from trust-constr. An equality constraint function must return zero at a feasible point:

\[ h(x)=0. \]

An inequality constraint function must return a nonnegative value at a feasible point:

\[ g(x)\geq0. \]

That is why the mathematical constraint \(x_0+2x_1\leq1\) becomes 1 - x[0] - 2*x[1] in the SLSQP dictionary. The expression is positive exactly when there is slack left.

Practical distinction: trust-constr asks you to state lower and upper limits directly. SLSQP asks you to write residual functions whose signs tell the solver whether each rule is satisfied.

How to choose between them

The right choice is less about which method is newer and more about what structure you can give the solver.

Method What it is trying to do Use it when Watch out for
trust-constr Choose a constrained step inside a trust region, balancing model decrease and feasibility. You have smooth constraints, want explicit lower and upper limits, or can exploit Hessians, Hessian-vector products, or sparse Jacobians. There are more moving parts to inspect: trust radius, constraint violation, barrier behavior, and derivative quality.
SLSQP Solve a sequence of quadratic programming approximations with equality and nonnegative-inequality residuals. The problem is small to medium sized, smooth, and easy to express with constraint dictionaries. The sign convention is easy to reverse by accident, and finite-difference Jacobians can be noisy if the functions are badly scaled.

Constraint Slack

Inequality constraints are often easiest to audit through slack. Suppose a constraint says \(c_j(x)\leq c_j^u\). The slack is the unused room before the upper limit:

\[ s_j(x)=c_j^u-c_j(x). \]

Now the sign has a clear meaning:

\[ \begin{aligned} s_j(x)>0 &\quad \text{constraint has room},\\ s_j(x)=0 &\quad \text{constraint is active},\\ s_j(x)<0 &\quad \text{constraint is violated}. \end{aligned} \]

This is a useful failed-shortcut check. If the objective value looks good but one slack is negative, the optimizer has not solved the constrained problem yet. It has found a low infeasible point.

Use Constrained Minimization In SciPy

The same mathematical problem can be represented with modern constraint objects for trust-constr, or with dictionary constraints for SLSQP. The code below names the objective gradient and the constraint Jacobians so that the pieces are visible instead of hidden inside lambdas. 

import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import Bounds, LinearConstraint, NonlinearConstraint, minimize

def rosen(x):
    return 100.0 * (x[1] - x[0]**2)**2 + (1.0 - x[0])**2

def rosen_der(x):
    return np.array([
        -400.0 * x[0] * (x[1] - x[0]**2) - 2.0 * (1.0 - x[0]),
        200.0 * (x[1] - x[0]**2),
    ])

def nonlinear_fun(x):
    return np.array([x[0]**2 + x[1], x[0]**2 - x[1]])

def nonlinear_jac(x):
    return np.array([
        [2.0 * x[0], 1.0],
        [2.0 * x[0], -1.0],
    ])

bounds = Bounds([0.0, -0.5], [1.0, 2.0])

linear = LinearConstraint(
    [[1.0, 2.0], [2.0, 1.0]],
    [-np.inf, 1.0],
    [1.0, 1.0],
)

nonlinear = NonlinearConstraint(
    nonlinear_fun,
    [-np.inf, -np.inf],
    [1.0, 1.0],
    jac=nonlinear_jac,
)

x0 = np.array([0.5, 0.0])

trust = minimize(
    rosen,
    x0=x0,
    method="trust-constr",
    jac=rosen_der,
    constraints=[linear, nonlinear],
    bounds=bounds,
)

def ineq_fun(x):
    return np.array([
        1.0 - x[0] - 2.0 * x[1],
        1.0 - x[0]**2 - x[1],
        1.0 - x[0]**2 + x[1],
    ])

def ineq_jac(x):
    return np.array([
        [-1.0, -2.0],
        [-2.0 * x[0], -1.0],
        [-2.0 * x[0], 1.0],
    ])

def eq_fun(x):
    return np.array([2.0 * x[0] + x[1] - 1.0])

def eq_jac(x):
    return np.array([[2.0, 1.0]])

ineq_cons = {
    "type": "ineq",
    "fun": ineq_fun,
    "jac": ineq_jac,
}

eq_cons = {
    "type": "eq",
    "fun": eq_fun,
    "jac": eq_jac,
}

slsqp = minimize(
    rosen,
    x0=x0,
    method="SLSQP",
    jac=rosen_der,
    constraints=[eq_cons, ineq_cons],
    bounds=[(0.0, 1.0), (-0.5, 2.0)],
)

print(trust.x, trust.fun)
print(slsqp.x, slsqp.fun)

Read the trust-constr setup

The bounds line is the variable box:

\[ 0\leq x_0\leq1,\qquad -0.5\leq x_1\leq2. \]

The LinearConstraint has two rows. The first row says \(x_0+2x_1\leq1\), because the lower limit is -np.inf and the upper limit is 1.0. The second row says \(2x_0+x_1=1\), because the lower and upper limits are both 1.0.

The NonlinearConstraint returns two nonlinear quantities. Each has no lower limit and an upper limit of 1.0, so both are one-sided inequalities:

\[ x_0^2+x_1\leq1,\qquad x_0^2-x_1\leq1. \]

The Jacobian nonlinear_jac tells the solver how those two quantities change when \(x_0\) or \(x_1\) changes. Its first row belongs to \(x_0^2+x_1\); its second row belongs to \(x_0^2-x_1\).

Read the SLSQP setup

The sign convention differs by interface. In LinearConstraint and NonlinearConstraint, you give lower and upper bounds directly. In an SLSQP inequality dictionary, the function should be nonnegative when the constraint is satisfied.

The conversion is mechanical once you know the sign convention:

Mathematical rule trust-constr expression SLSQP residual
\(x_0+2x_1\leq1\) upper limit 1.0 on row [1, 2] 1 - x[0] - 2*x[1]
\(2x_0+x_1=1\) lower and upper limits both 1.0 2*x[0] + x[1] - 1
\(x_0^2+x_1\leq1\) upper limit 1.0 on nonlinear component 1 - x[0]**2 - x[1]
\(x_0^2-x_1\leq1\) upper limit 1.0 on nonlinear component 1 - x[0]**2 + x[1]

This table also shows a common source of bugs. The same inequality appears with opposite-looking algebra because trust-constr stores the original left-hand side, while SLSQP stores the remaining slack.

Audit the result

After either call, do not stop at result.fun. A constrained result has two jobs: the objective should be low, and the constraints should be satisfied. A small audit can recompute the residuals in the same sign convention used by the problem: 

def feasibility_report(x):
    return {
        "linear_ineq_slack": 1.0 - x[0] - 2.0 * x[1],
        "nonlinear_ineq_slack": np.array([
            1.0 - x[0]**2 - x[1],
            1.0 - x[0]**2 + x[1],
        ]),
        "equality_residual": 2.0 * x[0] + x[1] - 1.0,
        "inside_bounds": np.all(np.logical_and(bounds.lb <= x, x <= bounds.ub)),
    }

print(feasibility_report(trust.x))
print(feasibility_report(slsqp.x))

Positive inequality slack means room remains. A near-zero equality residual means the equality line has been reached. If the objective is attractive but one of these checks fails, the point is not a valid constrained solution yet.

When To Use It

Use constrained minimization when Prefer another family when
The objective is smooth and the feasible set is described by bounds, equalities, or inequalities. The objective is naturally a residual vector; then least_squares may expose better structure.
You can provide analytic gradients or Jacobians for the objective and constraints. The feasible set is combinatorial or discrete; then linear, assignment, or mixed-integer tools may fit better.
Feasibility matters as much as objective reduction. The search space has many local minima and broad global exploration is the first concern.
Practical habit: inspect both result.fun and constraint violation. In constrained optimization, a pretty objective value is only meaningful when the point is feasible.
